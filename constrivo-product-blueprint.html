Constrivo

A homeowner-friendly trade coordination platform that keeps projects moving, trades informed, and homeowners updated automatically — built around readiness, communication, and proof, not just a calendar.

01 · Executive Summary

The two questions this product exists to answer

For a homeowner: "What is happening with my project?" For a trade: "Am I actually ready to go to this job?" Every feature in this blueprint is in service of answering one of those two questions automatically, instead of by phone call.

Constrivo is a trade coordination and homeowner communication platform — not a scheduling app. A scheduling app tells you a date. It cannot tell a trade that the framer isn't actually done, that the drywall hasn't been delivered, or that the homeowner hasn't been told a crew is coming tomorrow. Those are the failures that actually cost money: wasted trade trips, blindsided homeowners, and disputes that trace back to "nobody told me." Constrivo's core mechanic — a readiness check that runs before a trade is dispatched — is what a calendar structurally cannot do.

For homeowners

Always know who's coming, when, what's planned, why a delay happened, and what decision is waiting on them — in plain language, without calling to ask.

For contractors

One dashboard for today/tomorrow/late work, site-readiness issues, pending approvals, and daily reports — instead of being the manual relay between every trade and every homeowner.

For trades

Know before you drive whether the site is actually ready, get the scope and drawings in the invite itself, and prove completion with a photo instead of a phone call.

Why site readiness is the winning feature

Every other pain point in this document — wasted trips, homeowner surprise, disputed delays — traces back to one root cause: nobody checked whether the job was actually ready before someone showed up expecting to work. A readiness checklist that runs automatically before dispatch (Section 12) turns that root cause into a solved problem instead of a recurring one.

Why progress proof and automatic updates matter

Homeowner-contractor disputes are disproportionately caused by miscommunication and lack of transparency, not workmanship. Photo-backed daily reports and automatic, plain-language homeowner updates (Sections 13 & 15) remove the ambiguity that disputes grow in — there's a timestamped record of what happened and why, not two conflicting memories of a phone call.

What the MVP should focus on

Timeline, trade invite/confirm, the readiness checklist, mobile trade check-in/complete with photo, automatic homeowner updates, change order approval, and daily reports (Section 17). That's the full coordination loop — everything else waits.

What should wait

AI scheduling, deep accounting/payments, full municipal integrations, and a materials marketplace are all real value later, not launch requirements — see Section 22 for sequencing.

How to read this document
The five-dot meter at the top of this page is the platform's actual readiness-state vocabulary (Section 12) — Ready, At Risk, Not Ready, Waiting on Confirmation, Manual Review Needed. You'll see it reused throughout as the real status language, not decoration.

02 · Product Positioning

A coordination and communication platform, not a calendar

Product category

Construction coordination and homeowner communication platform.

Positioning statement

Constrivo helps contractors keep trades, clients, schedules, approvals, and site readiness aligned — automatically, not through another round of phone calls.

Tagline options

  • "Stop chasing trades. Stop guessing what happens next."
  • "Know if you're actually ready to go."
  • "Every trade informed. Every homeowner updated. Automatically."
  • "The site tells you when it's ready — not the other way around."

Homepage hero headline options

  • "Stop chasing trades. Stop guessing what happens next."
  • "Know your site is ready before anyone drives there."
  • "Homeowners informed. Trades ready. Contractors in control."

Homepage subheadline options

  • "Constrivo keeps homeowners, contractors, and trades connected from schedule to completion — with a readiness check before every dispatch."
  • "Automatic homeowner updates, a readiness check before every trade is dispatched, and photo-backed proof of what happened on site."

Main value proposition

Other tools schedule dates. Constrivo verifies readiness before a trade is dispatched, updates the homeowner automatically as real things happen, and produces a photo-backed record of every day on site — the three things that actually prevent wasted trips, surprised clients, and he-said-she-said disputes.

Differentiation statement

This is not scheduling software, calendar software, task management software, or a contractor messaging app. Those tools manage dates and threads. Constrivo verifies a real-world condition (is the site actually ready) before letting a dispatch happen, and that single mechanic is what the rest of the product — homeowner trust, trade adoption, dispute reduction — is built on top of.

Who it's for

Residential remodelers, ADU builders, and small-to-mid general contractors coordinating multiple trades per project who are currently doing that coordination by phone and text.

Who it's not for

Large commercial GCs who need heavyweight document control and enterprise PM (Procore's territory), or contractors running a single trade with no homeowner-facing complexity.

07 · Mobile App Structure

Built for a phone, in gloves, in the sun

Trade mobile navigation

Today · Upcoming · Job Details · Documents · Photos · Check In/Out · Issues · Messages · Payments/Invoices (if supported)

Field crew home screen

Today's assigned job · start time · address · site readiness status · scope summary · Navigate button · Call superintendent button · Check-in button · Delay/report issue button.

Mobile design requirements

  • Minimal typing — status, taps, and photos do almost all the work
  • Large buttons for gloved hands and quick glances
  • Fast load times — today's job is the first thing rendered
  • Offline tolerance — current job's data caches locally, actions queue and sync
  • Photo-first reporting for completion and issues
  • Tap-to-call, tap-to-navigate — no dialing, no address copy-paste
  • Clear status labels in plain language, using the Section 12 vocabulary consistently
  • SMS fallback for trades who won't install the app
  • No complex menus — one obvious next action per screen

A. Project Timeline

Purpose: one sequenced plan, rendered differently per audience. Problem solved: today the plan lives in the contractor's head; nobody else has a reliable view of it.

Workflow: contractor lays out phases → tasks get sequenced with dependencies and trade assignments → start/end dates set → delays recalculate downstream automatically.

Views: homeowner-friendly (plain-language phases, no internal task IDs), contractor detailed (full dependency graph, all trades), trade task view (only their own tasks and what's immediately before/after them).
Edge case: a delay on one task must recalculate downstream dates without silently changing a trade's already-confirmed date — confirmed assignments require explicit contractor review before shifting.
Success metric: % of projects where the homeowner never has to ask "what's the plan."

D. Trade Confirmation

Purpose: replace "call the sub and hope" with a trackable, binding state. Problem solved: verbal commitments aren't confirmed, logged, or backed by a fallback plan.

Workflow: job invite sent with scope/address/dates → trade accepts, declines, or proposes a new date → reminder sent before work → no-response triggers escalation to a backup trade or a contractor alert → contractor dashboard reflects real-time status.

Edge case: a trade accepts but doesn't actually have crew capacity — mitigated by a lightweight capacity confirmation step, not assumed from acceptance alone.
Success metric: % of confirmed jobs that result in an actual on-time arrival.

E. Photo Proof

Purpose: a visual, timestamped record that replaces "trust me, it's done." Problem solved: disputes over whether work was completed, or completed correctly, have no evidence to resolve them today.

Workflow: before/during/completion/issue photos, each tagged to a specific task → stored permanently in the project record → visible to the homeowner only if the contractor marks it visible (protects against showing an unflattering in-progress mess out of context).

Edge case: a trade uploads a photo of the wrong task — mitigated by requiring photo upload from within the specific task's screen, never a generic upload.
Success metric: % of completed tasks with photo evidence attached.

H. Delay and Issue Reporting

Purpose: make reporting a problem faster than making a phone call, so it actually gets used. Problem solved: delays get discovered by the homeowner or the next trade, not reported proactively.

Reason typeWho reportsWho's notifiedHomeowner-facing language

Site not readyTradeContractor"A prior step needs to finish before this can start."

Success metric: delay-reporting usage rate among active trades; homeowner complaint rate tied to delays.

I. Documents and Drawings

Purpose: make "which version is current" a solved problem. Workflow: upload drawings/specs → assign to specific tasks → version history retained → trades always see the latest revision, with an explicit "updated" alert if a revision lands mid-task → mobile view works offline once cached.
Permissions: trades see only documents relevant to their assigned tasks; homeowners see contract-level documents only, not internal specs.
Edge case: a task has no drawing linked at all — this should hard-warn the contractor before the task can be marked ready, not fail silently.
Success metric: rework incidents traced to an outdated document (target: trend to zero).

J. Payment or Invoice Status

Purpose: shared visibility into payment state without turning the platform into an accounting system. Workflow: payment milestones defined per project (contractor-facing) → homeowner sees milestone/amount/status tied to visible completed work → trade submits an invoice against a task, status moves submitted → approved → paid.
Visibility split: homeowners see payment milestones tied to their contract, never a trade's invoice amount; trades see their own invoice status only, never the homeowner's payment terms; contractor sees everything.
Edge case: this is explicitly not a full accounting/payment-processing system in MVP — status tracking only, no payment processing (see Section 17).
Success metric: days-to-payment for trades; reduction in "when do I get paid" calls.

12 · Trade Readiness Checklist System

The platform's actual differentiator

Before a trade is dispatched, the system checks whether the job is real — not just whether a date is on the calendar. This is the one mechanic a plain scheduling tool structurally cannot offer.

Readiness logic — what gets verified

  • Previous trade marked complete
  • Required photos uploaded
  • Materials delivered
  • Required inspection passed
  • Drawings/specs available
  • Site access confirmed
  • Parking/access instructions available
  • Homeowner notified
  • Weather acceptable
  • Required decision/change order approved

Readiness statuses

StatusMeaningTrade seesContractor sees
ReadyEvery checklist item clearsGreen light to dispatchNo action needed
At RiskNot blocked yet, but a dependency is close to slippingProceed, but a heads-up is shownFlagged for proactive attention
Not ReadyAt least one hard blocker is openHard warning before dispatchBlocking item, owner, and deadline shown
Waiting on ConfirmationDepends on an external party's response (trade acceptance, homeowner decision)N/A or "we're waiting on X"Who it's waiting on and since when
Manual Review NeededThe system can't determine readiness automatically (e.g., conflicting reports)"Contractor is confirming — hold for now"Flagged for a human decision

Readiness output per task

Readiness score/status · blocking items · recommended action · who owns the blocker · deadline · downstream impact (what else slips if this doesn't clear).

Contractor view

All not-ready tasks, reason, owner, downstream impact, a suggested fix, and one-tap Notify or Reschedule actions.

Trade view

A simple "ready to go" or "not ready" signal, the one missing item if blocked, who to contact, and the suggested next action — never the full internal checklist.

Homeowner view

Simplified, non-technical status only — "Your project is waiting on inspection," "Materials are scheduled for delivery," "Your contractor will update the schedule once this step is complete." Never internal blame or trade-to-trade conflict.

Non-negotiable safeguard
Never let a trade dispatch on a task marked Not Ready without an explicit contractor override — and log that override. The whole value of this feature collapses the first time a wasted trip happens despite a red status.

Architecture notes

Notification architecture

A single notification service owns channel selection (SMS/email/push/in-app), sensitive-message review gating, and delivery retries — not duplicated per feature.

Audit trail

Every readiness override, change-order approval, and status change writes an Audit Log row — this is what makes a dispute answerable months later.

Role-based access

Enforced at the API layer, not just hidden in the UI — a homeowner request can never resolve another homeowner's project data.

Mobile offline strategy

Current-task data (drawing, address, scope, readiness status) caches locally; check-in/out, photos, and reports queue locally and sync on reconnect.

Photo & document storage

Object storage with per-photo visibility flags enforced server-side, not client-side — a homeowner-hidden photo must never be reachable via a guessed URL.

API-first / integration readiness

Core actions (create task, confirm assignment, report delay, submit change order) are a stable API from day one — the web dashboard and mobile app are both just clients of it, and it's what Phase 4 integrations plug into.

18 · UX Principles

Rules that keep the product from becoming what it replaces

  • Mobile-first for trades — the field experience is the highest-leverage surface in the product
  • Homeowner-friendly language — no construction jargon, ever, in homeowner-facing copy
  • Contractor dashboard clarity — priority-ranked, not a flat list of everything
  • Minimal typing in the field — taps and photos do the work, free text is optional
  • Clear status labels — the five-state readiness vocabulary (§12) used consistently everywhere
  • Always show what happens next — never a dead-end status with no forward path
  • Reduce calls, don't create more work — every automated message must save a call, not add a task
  • Make readiness visible — a blocked task is always visible before someone drives to it
  • Avoid blame in homeowner communication — delay language is always neutral, never trade-vs-trade
  • Make approvals explicit — a decision is either approved or it isn't; no ambiguous middle state
  • Use photos as proof — every completion claim has visual evidence attached
  • Make delays understandable — a reason and a new date, every time, no exceptions
  • Make every sensitive automated message reviewable — delay explanations and change orders always pass through the contractor first

Final recommendation

Start with communication, readiness, and proof — not advanced scheduling. The readiness checklist and automatic homeowner updates are what make this feel different from a calendar on day one; a fancier scheduler doesn't matter if trades still show up to unready sites and homeowners still have to call to find out why.

Make trade adoption effortless — SMS-first, five taps or fewer for every core action, no forced app install. The coordination loop only works if trades actually use the check-in/photo/complete flow; if they don't, the readiness data going into the checklist goes stale and the whole differentiator collapses quietly.

Use homeowner communication as the clearest sales hook — it's the pain point every stakeholder (contractor, trade, and homeowner) has a personal stake in fixing, and it's the easiest thing to demo convincingly in a single screen.

Build the MVP around real jobs, not theoretical workflows — pilot on live projects, not sandbox demos, so the readiness checklist and delay-reporting logic get pressure-tested against how contractors actually work before wider release.

Add AI, deep integrations, and advanced scheduling only after the core coordination loop is trusted — those are compounding advantages, not launch requirements, and building them early risks distracting from the one mechanic (readiness before dispatch) that makes this product worth switching to.
