01 · Executive Summary

Stop calling it a scheduling app

A calendar tells you when something is supposed to happen. It does not tell you why plumbing is stalled because drywall hasn't been inspected, why the framer is idle because lumber is three days late, or why the homeowner is calling for the fourth time this week. That gap — between "the date on the calendar" and "what actually has to be true for the next trade to start" — is the entire product.

General contractors do not lose money because they lack a calendar. They lose money because handoffs fail silently: a trade finishes and nobody tells the next one, an inspection fails and nobody re-sequences downstream work, a delivery slips and the crew shows up to a site with no material. Every one of those failures currently gets caught — if it's caught at all — by a phone call. This platform replaces the phone call with a system that already knows the dependency existed.

That's why Construction Trade Coordination Platform is the right category name, and "scheduling app" or "project management tool" are both wrong. A scheduling app manages dates. A project management tool manages tasks and documents for the GC's own team. Neither one has a native concept of "a trade," "a downstream dependency," "a materials-blocking condition," or "a homeowner who needs a plain-English update." This product is built around exactly those four concepts, and everything else — the calendar view, the Gantt chart, the dashboard — is a rendering of the coordination graph, not the product itself.

The core workflow transformation

Today: GC manually re-derives "who needs to know what" every time something changes, by phone or text, from memory. Tomorrow: the dependency graph (schedule → trade → material → inspection → next trade → client) derives it automatically, and a human approves before anything customer-facing goes out.

Why the ecosystem matters

Standalone, this is "one more app trades have to check." Connected to Drawliner (takeoffs feed realistic durations) and ADUFlow (permit/municipality status becomes a real schedule gate), it becomes the only coordination layer that knows the true state of a project end-to-end — from quantity takeoff to final inspection. That data loop is very hard for a generic PM tool to replicate without owning the estimating and permitting layers too.

Biggest pain points this solves

  • No-shows and double-booking — trades commit to work they can't actually staff, and nobody finds out until the truck doesn't show up.
  • Silent handoff failures — the previous trade finishes (or doesn't) and the next trade is not automatically told either way.
  • Materials as an invisible dependency — schedules assume materials arrive on time; when they don't, nothing in the schedule reacts.
  • Inspection-gated work treated as calendar dates — a schedule says "drywall starts the 14th" when it should say "drywall starts when rough-in inspection passes," which is a different kind of fact.
  • Homeowners as an afterthought — every update is a manual phone call the GC has to remember to make, usually only after something's already gone wrong.

What the first version should focus on

Phase 1 is deliberately narrow: turn an accepted estimate into a real schedule, get trades to accept/decline on their phone, and make the "next trade" and "homeowner" notifications automatic and trustworthy. No AI autonomy, no municipal integration, no supplier EDI. See Section 16 — MVP Scope.

What makes this hard to copy

  1. The dependency graph itself — construction-specific sequencing logic (trade → inspection → material → trade) that generic PM tools don't model natively.
  2. Two-sided trust — trades must actually use the mobile flow (few taps, no learning curve) or the whole coordination graph collapses back to phone calls.
  3. The ecosystem data loop with Drawliner and ADUFlow — real quantities, real permit status, and real completion data compounding into better estimates and schedules over time.

03 · Product Positioning

A coordination operating system, not a calendar

Product category

Trade Coordination OS for construction — the connective layer between estimating, scheduling, trades, materials, inspections, and clients.

Positioning statement

For general contractors and builders who lose days to missed handoffs and phone tag, [Product] is the coordination operating system that automatically keeps every trade, supplier, inspector, and homeowner in sync — unlike scheduling apps and generic PM tools, which manage dates and tasks but not the dependencies between them.

Tagline options

  • "The schedule that tells the next trade when to show up."
  • "Stop being the phone call. Be the contractor."
  • "Every handoff, covered — automatically."
  • "Coordination, not calendars."

Homepage hero copy

Headline: "Nobody should find out a job is delayed by showing up to an empty site."
Subhead: [Product] connects your estimate, schedule, trades, materials, and inspections into one system — so when something changes, everyone who needs to know already does.

Main value proposition

Every other tool in this space asks the GC to keep managing coordination manually and just gives them a nicer place to write it down. This product removes the manual coordination step itself: dependencies are modeled once, and the system derives who needs to know what, every time something changes.

Differentiation messaging

vs. scheduling apps

A calendar app schedules dates. It has no concept of "this can't start until that inspection passes." This product's core unit is the dependency, not the date.

vs. project management software

PM tools (Asana, Monday) manage the GC's internal tasks. They have no native model of an external trade accepting/declining work, or a homeowner-facing view. This product is built for the three-sided relationship: GC, trade, client.

vs. calendar tools

Calendars show the plan. They can't detect that the plan is already broken (double-booked crew, late material, unapproved permit). Detection and automatic re-propagation is the product.

Why this fits with Drawliner and ADUFlow
Drawliner produces the quantities that should drive realistic task durations; ADUFlow produces the permit and municipality status that should gate the schedule. Neither currently has anywhere to feed that data into — this product is that destination, and it becomes measurably better every time a project runs through all three.

A. Job & Project Creation

Purpose: establish the single record everything else attaches to. Problem solved: project data currently starts scattered (CRM, email, memory) and never converges.

Workflow: create manually or from an accepted estimate → link client/homeowner → link address (validated, structured) → link drawings → link scope of work → link budget → link permit status (if applicable) → assign PM → generate baseline schedule.

Required fields: project name, address, client contact, project type, start-date target. Edge cases: multi-unit or multi-address projects (e.g., subdivisions); re-opening a "complete" project for warranty work.
Success metric: time from "estimate accepted" to "baseline schedule exists," target under 5 minutes.

B. Estimate-to-Schedule Conversion

Purpose: turn what was priced into what gets built and when, without manual re-entry. Problem solved: estimates and schedules are built by different people in different tools today, so they silently drift apart.

Workflow: estimate accepted → scope line items become project tasks → takeoff quantities (from Drawliner, if connected) generate estimated durations via a duration-per-unit model → cost codes map to schedule activities → trade categories map to available subcontractors → dependencies are generated from a standard sequencing template per project type → materials are identified per task → inspection milestones are inserted at the correct points → critical path is calculated.

Edge case
Line items that don't map cleanly to a standard trade/sequence template (custom scope, unusual project types) must fall back to a manual sequencing step — never silently guess a dependency order.

Success metric: % of estimate line items that convert to a scheduled task without manual correction.

D. Trade Invitations

Purpose: replace the "call the sub and hope" step with a trackable, binding invitation. Problem solved: verbal commitments aren't confirmed, aren't logged, and give the GC no fallback plan when they fall through.

Workflow: invite trade to task → send scope, drawings, address, and site instructions in the same invite → trade accepts, declines, or proposes an alternate date → GC approves or counters the alternate → trade confirms crew capacity for that date → confirmation propagates to PM, superintendent, and (if it's the next trade in sequence) the previous trade who was waiting on this handoff.

UX requirement: a trade should be able to accept a straightforward invite in under 15 seconds from a push notification, without opening a full app session.
Edge case: invitation expires with no response — must auto-escalate to a backup trade or alert the PM, never sit silently.
Success metric: median time-to-accept; % of invitations that expire unanswered.

G. Drawing & Document Access

Purpose: make "which version is current" a solved problem. Problem solved: drawings emailed once go stale silently after revisions; trades work from outdated PDFs.

Workflow: drawings link to specific tasks, not just the project → viewing always resolves to the latest revision → prior revisions remain in version history for reference → a task shows a hard warning if no drawing is linked at all → offline caching for the current task's drawing so a trade isn't stuck without signal on site.

Integration: Bluebeam Revu and Drawliner as source-of-truth for takeoffs/markups; this platform is a consumer/viewer, not a competing markup tool in V1.
Edge case: a revision lands mid-task — the trade already on site needs an explicit "drawing updated" alert, not a silent swap.
Success metric: rework incidents attributable to outdated drawings (target: trend to zero).

J. Client / Homeowner Communication

Purpose: make "the homeowner already knows" the default state, not something the GC has to remember. Problem solved: today, communication quality depends entirely on the GC's bandwidth on a given day.

Workflow: homeowner dashboard shows project timeline, upcoming crew visits, milestones completed with photos, and current delays → milestone completion auto-drafts a plain-language update → PM reviews/edits/approves before it sends (never fully automatic to a client, see Section 12 safeguards) → homeowner sets communication preference (SMS, email, or both) and frequency.

Edge case: bad news (a real delay) needs faster, more direct notice than routine milestones — don't batch a delay into a weekly digest.
Success metric: inbound "what's happening" calls/texts per project, trending down.

K. Notifications & Alerts

Purpose: the mechanism that actually replaces the phone call. Problem solved: today, "telling people" is a manual, error-prone, one-at-a-time act.

07 · Smart Scheduler Design

The engine underneath everything

The scheduler's job is narrow: turn known facts (scope, durations, dependencies, availability, constraints) into a defensible sequence — and never publish a change without a human seeing what moved and why.

Inputs

  • Project type & scope of work
  • Estimate line items & takeoff quantities
  • Trade durations (historical + takeoff-derived)
  • Task dependencies (predecessor/successor)
  • Crew availability
  • Material availability / lead times
  • Permit status
  • Inspection requirements
  • Weather forecast (for weather-sensitive tasks)
  • Holidays / blackout dates
  • Builder preferences (e.g., no Friday finishes)
  • Client constraints (access windows)
  • Site access restrictions

Outputs

  • Baseline project schedule
  • Task sequence with dependencies
  • Trade assignments (proposed, pending acceptance)
  • Critical path
  • Start/end dates per task
  • Buffer days around weather/inspection-sensitive tasks
  • Inspection request dates
  • Material delivery windows
  • Notification triggers
  • Delay impact forecast (what a slip does downstream)

Scheduling logic

FactorHandling rule
DependenciesHard gates (task cannot start) vs. soft preferences (should follow, but can overlap) are modeled separately — most construction sequencing is hard-gated.
Crew availabilityScheduler only proposes trades who are marked available for that window; it never force-assigns a busy trade.
Trade capacityA trade can decline based on crew size vs. scope — capacity is self-reported by the trade, not assumed by the system.
Weather-sensitive workExterior/weather-sensitive tasks get an automatic buffer and a forecast check before the confirmed date locks in.
InspectionsInspection-gated tasks cannot have a downstream start date before the inspection is requested; the actual downstream start is not confirmed until the inspection is recorded as passed.
PermitsNo task requiring an active permit can be scheduled to start before permit approval is recorded.
Material deliveryA task cannot show as "ready" until its linked material delivery date has passed or been confirmed on-site.
HolidaysExcluded from availability calculations by default per region/org calendar.
Double-bookingPrevented at the assignment layer — a trade cannot be confirmed on two overlapping tasks across any project in the platform.
Manual overridesAlways allowed, but any override that breaks a hard dependency triggers a visible warning that must be acknowledged before saving.
DelaysA reported delay recalculates every downstream task's proposed dates; it does not silently recalculate confirmed trade assignments without notifying the affected trade.
Recalculating future tasksOnly tasks that are not yet confirmed/in-progress shift automatically; confirmed near-term work requires explicit PM review before changing.

Required safeguards

  • Human approval is required before any schedule change affecting a confirmed trade assignment goes live.
  • Every proposed change shows a plain-language change summary ("Framing pushed 2 days — rough-in inspection was delayed") before publishing.
  • Every affected party is notified specifically, not just "the project changed."
  • Every automatic adjustment is written to an audit log with its triggering event.
  • Manual overrides that violate a hard dependency show an explicit warning naming the dependency being broken.
  • Critical path impact is shown before a change is confirmed, not just after.

Coordination logic

Preventing double-booking

Assignment is a hard lock across the entire platform, not per-project — a trade confirmed on one job cannot be confirmed on an overlapping job anywhere in the system.

Handling late trades

A grace period after the scheduled start with no check-in triggers a "running late?" nudge to the trade, then an alert to the PM if still unconfirmed.

Handling no-shows

No check-in by end of the grace window auto-flags a no-show, notifies the PM immediately, and offers one-tap re-invite to a backup trade.

Reassigning work

Reassignment re-runs the same invitation flow (Function D) with the original scope/drawings/address pre-filled — no re-entry.

Notifying downstream trades

A no-show or delay immediately re-triggers the "next trade" notification logic in reverse — downstream trades are told to hold, not left to assume the schedule still stands.

Keeping it light for trades

Accountability comes from visible status (check-in/out, completion), not from forms or approvals — the trade's entire required workflow should fit in under five taps per visit.

Handling material-specific realities

Concrete

Weather- and cure-time-sensitive; scheduler should treat pour date as a hard gate with a mandatory cure buffer before the next trade.

Framing lumber

Price/availability volatility makes early ordering valuable; flag long lead times against the framing start date automatically.

Windows

Classic long-lead item (often 4-8+ weeks) — should be ordered at estimate acceptance, not at "framing complete."

Roofing materials

Weather-window sensitive; pair with the weather-aware scheduling logic in Section 7.

Drywall

Depends on rough-in inspection passing first — model as inspection-gated, not just material-gated.

Electrical/plumbing rough-in materials

Often trade-supplied rather than GC-ordered; the platform should support "trade brings own materials" as a valid state with no delivery tracking required.

Long-lead items and backorders: flagged automatically against required-by date at order time, not discovered later. Delivery rescheduling: always re-runs the same dependency recalculation as a task delay (Section 7) — materials are not a separate system from the schedule.

10 · Permits, Inspections & Municipality Integration

Where ADUFlow makes this platform hard to copy

Permit workflow

Submitted → under review → corrections required → approved → (eventually) expired. Documents attach to the permit record. Permit status is a real schedule gate: any task requiring an active permit cannot be scheduled to start before "approved" is recorded (Section 7).

Inspection workflow

Required → requested → scheduled → inspector assigned → passed / failed. A failed inspection creates a correction task automatically and blocks downstream trades until re-inspection passes — it does not just log the failure and move on.

Municipality integration as a differentiator

Most competitors treat inspections as a line on a calendar the GC fills in manually. Because ADUFlow already talks to municipalities for permitting, this platform can be the first to make inspection status a live, two-way fact instead of a guess:

  • Permit status syncs automatically instead of being manually re-checked
  • Inspections can be requested from inside the same tool that holds the schedule
  • A required-inspection checklist is enforced before a request can even be submitted
  • Inspectors and contractors get symmetric notifications from one event, not two separate phone calls
  • Pass/fail automatically updates the schedule — no one has to remember to "go tell the framer"
  • A municipal-facing dashboard (later phase) gives inspectors a queue instead of a voicemail box
Non-negotiable safeguards
Never assume an inspection passed without an explicit recorded result. Never notify the next trade until the pass condition is actually met. Any manual override of a gate must be logged with who and why. A failed inspection must generate a correction task automatically. Homeowner-facing language about inspections must stay non-technical and calm — "inspector asked for one more check on the electrical panel," not "failed 220-volt subpanel clearance inspection."

11 · Mobile-First Field App Design

Built for a phone, in gloves, in the sun

If a crew member can't complete their entire required workflow in a few taps with one hand, they revert to texting — and the whole coordination graph collapses back to phone calls. This is the highest-leverage design surface in the product.

Required mobile actions

Accept work · decline work · delay work · view drawings · view address · navigate to site · call superintendent · check in · check out · upload photos · mark complete · add notes · request inspection · report issue · view next task · receive schedule updates.

Mobile UX requirements

  • Large touch targets — designed for gloved hands and quick glances, not precision tapping
  • Minimal typing — status changes, photos, and taps do almost all the work; free text is optional, never required to proceed
  • Offline tolerance — the current task's drawing, address, and instructions cache locally; check-in/out and photos queue and sync when signal returns
  • Fast loading — today's task list is the first thing rendered, before anything else fetches
  • Simple task list — one list, one obvious next action per item, no nested navigation to find "what do I do"
  • Clear status labels in plain language ("Waiting on inspection," not "Blocked: dependency unresolved")
  • Photo upload as a first-class, one-tap action from the task screen itself
  • Voice note option for delay/issue reports, for crews who'd rather talk than type
  • One-tap navigation handoff to the phone's native maps app
  • One-tap call to the superintendent or PM, no dialing
  • SMS fallback for any push notification, since field connectivity and OS permissions are unreliable
  • A single, unmistakable "what do I do next" screen as the app's home state

Architecture notes

Realtime updates

Task and Assignment status changes push to affected clients (mobile + dashboard) via a realtime channel — polling alone is too slow for "who's on site right now."

Notification system

A single notification service owns channel selection (push/SMS/email), quiet-hours and opt-out logic, and delivery retries — not duplicated per feature.

Audit trail

Every automated schedule change, AI recommendation acceptance, and permission-sensitive action writes to the Audit Log — this is what makes "why did this change" answerable months later.

Offline mobile

Current-task data (drawing, address, scope) caches locally; check-in/out, photos, and reports queue locally and sync on reconnect, with conflict resolution favoring the field-reported timestamp.

Data sync & integration architecture

External integrations (Drawliner, ADUFlow, QuickBooks, suppliers) go through a dedicated integration layer with per-source retry/backoff — a slow or down third party should degrade a feature, not the core app.

API-first approach

Core actions (create task, accept assignment, report delay, request inspection) are exposed as a stable API from day one — the mobile app and web dashboard are both just clients of it, and it's what future ecosystem integrations plug into.

Security and privacy: role-scoped data access enforced at the API layer (not just hidden in the UI), least-privilege admin access with logging, and encryption in transit and at rest for anything containing client PII or financial data.

19 · Go-To-Market Strategy

Start where the pain is sharpest and the crew count is small enough to see results fast

Ideal initial customers

Small general contractors and residential builders running multiple trades per project on a repeatable workflow — remodelers, ADU builders, and trade-heavy contractors already frustrated with phone/text coordination.

Recommended beachhead

ADU and small residential remodel builders: high trade-coordination density per project, a natural on-ramp to the ADUFlow relationship, and short enough project cycles to prove ROI in weeks, not quarters.

  • "Stop calling every trade manually."
  • "Know who is on site tomorrow — before tomorrow."
  • "The next trade finds out automatically. You don't have to remember to tell them."

  • "Homeowners stay informed without you making another call."
  • "An accepted estimate becomes a real schedule — automatically."

Launch strategy

  1. Pilot with 3–5 contractors on real, live projects — not a sandbox demo.

Final recommendation

Build this as a shared coordination engine inside the broader ecosystem, not a fully standalone product. Its data model (Section 15) is designed to consume Drawliner's quantities and ADUFlow's permit/inspection status as first-class inputs — value that a standalone competitor structurally cannot replicate without also owning estimating and permitting. That's what makes the ecosystem approach more defensible than shipping "just another scheduling app": the moat isn't a feature, it's the compounding data loop across three connected products.

What reinforces what: Drawliner makes this platform's schedules more accurate from day one of a project; ADUFlow makes its inspection gating real instead of guessed; this platform, in turn, is where both of those products' outputs finally get used to change what actually happens on site — and its completion data flows back to make the next estimate and the next permit timeline better too.

Build first: the coordination MVP (Section 16) standalone, so it delivers value even before any ecosystem connection exists — that's what proves trade-side adoption, which is the hardest and most important risk to retire early (Section 20).
Wait on: AI autonomy, full municipal integration, and marketplace features — all three are more valuable once there's a real, adopted coordination graph to plug them into, and all three are expensive to get wrong before that.
Avoid: chasing feature-parity with Procore/Buildertrend on breadth. Win on depth in the one place they're both structurally weak — the actual moment-to-moment coordination between GC, trade, and homeowner.
